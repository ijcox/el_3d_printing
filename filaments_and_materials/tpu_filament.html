TPU Filament

Thermoplastic Polyurethane (TPU) is a flexible filament that bridges the gap between rigid plastics and rubber. With its unique combination of elasticity, durability, and shock absorption, TPU opens up new possibilities for functional prints that need to bend, flex, or absorb impacts.

What Is TPU?

TPU is a class of polyurethane plastics that combines the processing advantages of thermoplastics with the elastic properties of rubber. It consists of both hard and soft segments in its molecular structure, giving it a unique balance of flexibility and strength.

The Flexible Problem-Solver: TPU can stretch, compress, and return to its original shape, making it ideal for protective cases, seals, gaskets, and any application where flexibility and impact resistance are more important than rigidity.

TPU Variants & Special Formulations

Standard TPU (85A-95A)

General purpose flexible filament. Good balance of flexibility and printability. Most common for phone cases, protective covers, and flexible joints.

High-Performance TPU

Enhanced formulations with better layer adhesion, higher temperature resistance, or improved chemical resistance for demanding applications.

Polyester-based TPU

Better hydrolysis resistance, UV stability, and low-temperature flexibility. Often used for outdoor applications.

Polyether-based TPU

Superior flexibility at low temperatures, better microbial resistance. Common in medical and marine applications.

Color-Changing TPU

Thermochromic or photochromic variants that change color with temperature or UV light exposure.

Composite TPU

Mixed with materials like carbon fiber or glass fiber to increase stiffness while maintaining some flexibility.

Printing TPU: Challenges & Solutions

⚠️ Extruder Design is Critical: TPU's flexibility makes it challenging to feed through many extruder designs. Bowden tube setups are particularly problematic.

Extruder Type

Direct Drive: Highly recommended. Short path from gears to nozzle minimizes compression.
Bowden: Difficult but possible with slow speeds, perfectly calibrated retraction, and low friction tubes.

Retraction Settings

Minimal Retraction: 1-3mm maximum
Slow Speed: 20-30 mm/s
Excessive retraction can cause jams as TPU compresses in the hotend

Print Speed

Slow is Key: 15-30 mm/s for best results
First Layer: 10-15 mm/s
Travel Speed: 80-100 mm/s to minimize stringing

Temperature Control

Nozzle: 220-230°C typical
Bed: 50-60°C (not strictly required but helps)
No Cooling Fan or minimal (10-20% max)

Pro Tip: If using a Bowden setup, print a "flexible filament helper" - a small piece that guides filament straight into the extruder gears, preventing it from buckling.

Optimal Printing Settings

Slicer Settings

Layer Height: 0.2-0.3mm
Line Width: 0.4-0.5mm (wider is better)
Infill: 20-50% (gyroid works well)
Walls: 2-3 (more walls = less flexible)

Adhesion & Bed

Surface: PEI, BuildTak, or glass with glue stick
First Layer: 120-150% flow rate
Z-offset: Slightly closer than normal for good squish

Flow & Extrusion

Flow Rate: 95-105% (calibrate for your filament)
Pressure Advance: If available, helps with corners
Coasting: Enable to reduce blobs

Supports

Avoid if possible - very difficult to remove
If needed: Tree supports with large interface gap
Orientation: Design to minimize overhangs

Strengths of TPU

  • Exceptional Flexibility & Elasticity: Can stretch and return to original shape repeatedly without permanent deformation.
  • Outstanding Impact Resistance: Absorbs shocks and vibrations better than any rigid filament.
  • Excellent Abrasion Resistance: Wears well in high-friction applications, often outperforming nylon and PETG.
  • Good Chemical Resistance: Resists oils, greases, and many chemicals.
  • Water Resistance: Naturally waterproof - ideal for seals, gaskets, and marine applications.
  • Excellent Layer Adhesion: Layers bond strongly, creating durable, flexible parts.
  • Transparency Options: Can be printed in clear/translucent forms for see-through flexible parts.
  • Wide Temperature Range: Remains flexible from -40°C to +80°C in many formulations.

Limitations & Considerations

  • Challenging to Print: Requires specific extruder setup and slow print speeds.
  • Stringing & Oozing: Prone to stringing due to its flexible nature.
  • Poor Dimensional Accuracy: Flexible nature means parts may not hold tight tolerances.
  • Supports Are Difficult: Removing supports from flexible prints often damages the part.
  • Limited Heat Resistance: Softens at relatively low temperatures (60-80°C).
  • Moisture Absorption: Absorbs moisture from air, affecting print quality if not stored properly.
  • Not Food Safe: Even food-grade TPU isn't recommended for prolonged food contact.
  • Higher Cost: Typically more expensive than PLA or PETG.

Ideal Applications

TPU excels where flexibility, shock absorption, or rubber-like properties are needed.

Protective Cases

Phone cases, tablet covers, camera housings, and electronic enclosures that absorb impacts.

Wearables & Grips

Watch bands, helmet liners, tool handles, and ergonomic grips that conform to shapes.

Seals & Gaskets

Custom O-rings, door seals, waterproof gaskets, and plumbing applications.

Medical & Rehabilitation

Prosthetic liners, orthopedic devices, and cushioning elements for medical equipment.

Robotics & Drones

Flexible joints, vibration dampeners, protective bumpers, and compliant mechanisms.

Shoe Insoles & Sports

Custom insoles, cleat covers, sports equipment padding, and performance gear.

Post-Processing & Finishing

Trimming & Cleaning

Use sharp scissors or flush cutters to remove strings and supports. X-Acto knives work well for fine trimming.

Surface Finishing

Difficult to sand due to flexibility. Can be smoothed with careful application of a heat gun (brief exposure).

Bonding & Assembly

Bonds well with cyanoacrylate (super glue), epoxy, or specialized polyurethane adhesives. Can be stitched or riveted.

Painting

Requires flexible primer and flexible paint (like paints for vinyl or rubber). Standard paints will crack when flexed.

Design Tip: When designing for TPU, include fillets and rounded corners. Sharp corners create stress concentrations that can lead to tearing when the part is flexed.
