PLA Filament

Polylactic Acid (PLA) is the most popular and beginner-friendly material in FDM/FFF 3D printing. Derived from renewable resources like corn starch or sugarcane, it's easy to print, has a pleasant smell, and comes in a vast array of colors and blends.

What Is PLA?

PLA is a biodegradable thermoplastic polymer made from fermented plant sugars. It's a rigid material with low thermal resistance but excellent printability, making it the default choice for hobbyists, educators, and anyone new to 3D printing.

Perfect for Beginners: PLA prints at relatively low temperatures, doesn't require a heated bed (though it helps), has minimal warping, and is widely available at an affordable price.

Key Properties & Characteristics

Property Description
Print Temperature 190°C - 220°C (Nozzle)
Bed Temperature 50°C - 70°C (Optional, but recommended)
Strength & Rigidity High stiffness, but relatively brittle under impact
Heat Resistance Low (~50-60°C). Parts can deform in a hot car or dishwasher.
Warping/Shrinkage Very low. Excellent dimensional stability during printing.
Odor Slight, sweet smell (like pancakes or syrup). Non-toxic fumes.
Biodegradability Yes, under industrial composting conditions (not in a home compost).

Specialty PLA Variants

Basic PLA has evolved into numerous specialty filaments that maintain PLA's easy printing characteristics while adding unique properties.

PLA+ / Pro PLA

Enhanced formulations with added polymers or modifiers for increased strength, layer adhesion, and impact resistance while maintaining easy printability.

Silk / Metallic PLA

Contains additives that give prints a shiny, silky, or metallic luster. Aesthetic filament for decorative models. Can be more brittle.

Wood-Filled PLA

Infused with wood fibers (e.g., bamboo, cedar). Prints look and smell like wood and can be sanded, stained, or varnished.

Glow-in-the-Dark PLA

Contains strontium aluminate pigments. Requires a hardened steel nozzle due to its abrasive nature. Charges under light.

Color-Changing PLA

Thermochromic (changes with temperature) or photochromic (changes with UV light) pigments create dynamic visual effects.

Carbon Fiber / Fiberglass PLA

Reinforced with short fibers for dramatically increased stiffness and reduced weight. Highly abrasive; requires a hardened nozzle.

Optimal Printing Settings & Tips

  • Temperature Tuning: Always perform a temperature tower test. Start at 200°C and adjust ±10°C based on results. Silk and matte PLAs often need higher temps (~215-225°C).
  • Bed Adhesion: Use a clean, slightly heated bed (60°C). For stubborn prints, apply a thin layer of glue stick, hairspray, or use a PEI sheet.
  • Cooling is Critical: Use 100% part cooling fan speed after the first few layers. This is essential for sharp details and successful overhangs.
  • Print Speed: 40-80 mm/s works well. Slow down for small details or intricate models.
  • Retraction: Typical settings: 4-7mm distance at 25-45 mm/s. Prevents stringing and blobs.
Moisture is the Enemy: PLA is hygroscopic (absorbs moisture from the air). Wet filament leads to popping sounds, poor layer adhesion, and surface defects. Store PLA in a sealed container with desiccant when not in use.

Strengths of PLA

  • Incredibly Easy to Print: Forgiving with settings, minimal warping, works on almost any printer.
  • Wide Availability & Low Cost: The most common filament, available in thousands of colors and brands at affordable prices.
  • Excellent Detail & Surface Finish: Low melting temperature allows for sharp corners and fine features with minimal stringing.
  • Safe & Low Odor: Can be printed in living spaces without heavy ventilation (though good airflow is still recommended).
  • Biodegradable: An eco-friendly choice when sourced from renewable materials (though it requires specific conditions to degrade).

Limitations of PLA

  • Low Heat Resistance: Not suitable for parts exposed to warmth (e.g., car interiors, near electronics, outdoors in summer sun).
  • Brittleness: Can snap or crack under impact or stress, rather than bending. Not ideal for high-stress functional parts.
  • Poor Chemical Resistance: Degrades when exposed to many common solvents and chemicals.
  • Outdoor Degradation: UV light and moisture will cause it to become brittle and degrade over time.
  • Not Truly "Compostable" at Home: Requires industrial composting facilities (50-60°C) for timely breakdown.

Ideal Applications

PLA is perfect for projects where appearance, ease of printing, and low cost are priorities over mechanical strength and heat resistance.

Visual Prototypes & Models

Display models, architectural models, and concept prototypes where form is more important than function.

Decorative Objects

Vases, figurines, art pieces, cosplay props (non-structural), and household decorations.

Educational Projects

The standard for classrooms and workshops due to its safety, ease of use, and minimal equipment requirements.

Low-Stress Tools & Organizers

Drawer dividers, cable holders, templates, and simple jigs that aren't subjected to force or heat.

Post-Processing PLA Prints

  • Sanding: Start with coarse grit (120-220) and work up to fine grit (800-2000) for a smooth finish. Sand wet to avoid clogging paper and inhaling dust.
  • Priming & Painting: Use a filler primer to hide layer lines, then paint with acrylics or spray paints. PLA sands and paints very well.
  • Polishing: Can be flame-polished (carefully with a torch) or chemically smoothed with specific solvents in vapor baths (experimental).
  • Assembling: Use CA glue (super glue), epoxy, or PLA-specific adhesives for strong bonds.
