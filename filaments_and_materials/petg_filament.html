PETG Filament

PETG (Polyethylene Terephthalate Glycol-modified) is the perfect balance between PLA's ease of printing and ABS's durability. Known for its strength, chemical resistance, and reliability, it's become the go-to material for functional parts that need to withstand real-world use.

What Is PETG?

PETG is a glycol-modified version of PET (the plastic used in water bottles). The glycol modification reduces brittleness and improves printability while maintaining excellent mechanical properties. It's less prone to warping than ABS, stronger than PLA, and offers good layer adhesion.

The Functional Workhorse: PETG excels at creating durable, heat-resistant, and chemically resistant parts that can handle stress, impacts, and environmental exposure better than standard PLA.

Key Properties & Characteristics

Property Description
Print Temperature 230°C - 260°C (Nozzle)
Bed Temperature 70°C - 85°C (Required)
Strength & Flexibility High impact resistance with some flex, less brittle than PLA
Heat Resistance Good (~75-85°C). Can withstand warm environments better than PLA.
Chemical Resistance Excellent resistance to water, acids, alkalis, and many chemicals
Warping/Shrinkage Minimal (much less than ABS), but more than PLA
Odor Minimal to none during printing
Moisture Absorption Moderate. Requires dry storage but less sensitive than nylon.

Optimal Printing Settings & Techniques

Temperature Control

Nozzle: 240-250°C is typical sweet spot. Bed: 75-80°C for good adhesion. Print in an enclosure for large parts to reduce warping.

Cooling Strategy

Use minimal to no part cooling fan for the first layers, then 20-50% for the rest. Too much cooling reduces layer adhesion and can cause warping.

Print Speed

40-60 mm/s is optimal. Slow down for first layers (20-30 mm/s) and complex details. PETG needs time to bond properly.

Retraction & Stringing

PETG is prone to stringing. Use moderate retraction: 4-6mm distance at 40-50 mm/s. Enable coasting and wiping in slicer settings.

Nozzle Gap is Critical: PETG needs a slightly larger first layer nozzle gap than PLA (0.1-0.2mm higher). PETG sticks too well to the bed and can damage it (or itself) if squished too much.
Bed Adhesion Secret: Clean glass or PEI sheets work great. Apply a thin layer of glue stick or hairspray as a release agent—PETG can fuse to bare glass.

Strengths of PETG

  • Excellent Layer Adhesion: Parts are strong and durable with good inter-layer bonding.
  • Chemical & Water Resistance: Ideal for parts exposed to liquids, chemicals, or outdoor conditions.
  • Good Flexibility & Impact Resistance: Can bend and absorb impacts without shattering like PLA.
  • Food Safe (When Properly Processed): Many PETG filaments are FDA-approved for food contact once properly cleaned and cured.
  • UV Resistance: Better than PLA for outdoor applications that see sunlight.
  • Recyclable: Same recycling stream as PET plastic bottles.

Limitations & Challenges

  • Stringing & Oozing: More prone to stringing than PLA, requiring careful retraction tuning.
  • Bed Adhesion Can Be Too Good: Can fuse to glass beds, potentially damaging both print and bed if not using a release agent.
  • Not as Stiff as PLA: More flexible, which is good for some applications but not ideal for rigid structural parts.
  • Moisture Sensitive: Absorbs moisture from air which causes printing defects and reduced strength. Requires dry storage.
  • Higher Print Temperatures: Requires an all-metal hotend for prolonged printing above 245°C.

Ideal Applications

PETG shines where durability, chemical resistance, and reliability are more important than perfect surface finish.

Functional Prototypes

Parts that need to withstand testing, assembly, and real-world handling.

Mechanical Parts

Gears, brackets, mounts, and tools that require strength and durability.

Outdoor & Wet Environments

Planters, garden tools, outdoor fixtures, and parts exposed to weather.

Containers & Enclosures

Electronic cases, tool holders, and storage bins that benefit from chemical resistance.

Food-Safe Items

Cups, utensils, and containers (after proper cleaning and surface treatment).

Replacement Parts

DIY repairs around the house, garage, or workshop where durability matters.

Post-Processing PETG Prints

  • Sanding: More difficult than PLA but possible. Use coarse grit to start and work up gradually.
  • Glueing: Bonds well with CA glue (super glue) or epoxy. PETG-specific adhesives also available.
  • Annealing: Can be annealed at 70-80°C for 30-60 minutes to increase heat resistance and strength.
  • Painting: Requires sanding and priming first. PETG doesn't take paint as easily as PLA.
  • Cleaning: Isopropyl alcohol works well for removing glue stick residue from prints.
