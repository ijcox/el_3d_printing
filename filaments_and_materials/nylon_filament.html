Nylon Filament

Nylon (Polyamide) is a premium engineering thermoplastic renowned for its exceptional strength, durability, and chemical resistance. While challenging to print, it produces parts that can replace metal in many applications, making it the choice for high-performance functional components.

What Is Nylon Filament?

Nylon is a family of synthetic polymers (polyamides) known for their outstanding mechanical properties. In 3D printing, Nylon 6 and Nylon 6/6 are most common, offering high strength-to-weight ratios, excellent layer adhesion, and natural flexibility that resists cracking under stress.

The Professional's Material: Nylon bridges the gap between consumer filaments and industrial polymers. It's used for gears, bearings, mechanical assemblies, and functional prototypes that demand durability beyond what PLA, PETG, or ABS can offer.

Key Properties & Characteristics

Property Description
Print Temperature 240°C - 280°C (Nozzle)
Bed Temperature 70°C - 100°C (Required)
Strength & Toughness Exceptional impact resistance, high tensile strength, natural flexibility
Heat Resistance Good (~80-100°C). Higher than PETG, lower than ABS.
Chemical Resistance Excellent resistance to oils, fuels, and many chemicals
Warping Moderate to High - Requires careful temperature management
Moisture Absorption Extremely High - Will absorb water from air within hours
Layer Adhesion Excellent - Layers fuse together almost indistinguishably
Abrasion Resistance Outstanding - Self-lubricating, ideal for moving parts

Nylon Variants & Composites

Nylon is often enhanced with additives to improve specific properties for demanding applications.

Nylon 6

Good balance of properties, easier to print than Nylon 6/6. Most common in filament form. Good impact strength and flexibility.

Nylon 6/6

Higher temperature resistance and stiffness than Nylon 6. More challenging to print. Industry standard for injection molding.

Carbon Fiber Nylon

Nylon reinforced with carbon fibers. Increased stiffness, reduced warping, improved dimensional stability. Abrasive - requires hardened nozzle.

Glass Fiber Nylon

Improved stiffness and heat resistance compared to pure nylon. Less abrasive than carbon fiber but still requires hardened nozzle.

Nylon Co-polymers

Modified nylons (like Taulman's alloys) with improved printability, reduced moisture absorption, or enhanced flexibility.

Nylon with MOF

Metal-Organic Frameworks added to reduce moisture absorption - significantly extends filament shelf life.

Critical Printing Challenges

⚠️ Moisture Absorption - The Biggest Challenge

Problem: Nylon absorbs 5-10% of its weight in water from humid air within 24 hours. Wet nylon causes severe printing defects:

  • Popping, sizzling, and steam during printing
  • Poor layer adhesion and weak parts
  • Surface bubbles and roughness
  • Dimensional inaccuracy

Solutions:

  • Dry before printing: 4-8 hours at 70-80°C in a filament dryer or food dehydrator
  • Print from dry box: Continuous drying during printing is essential
  • Store with desiccant: Vacuum-sealed bags with silica gel when not in use
  • Consider nylon alloys: Some co-polymers have reduced moisture absorption

⚠️ Warping & Adhesion Issues

Problem: Nylon contracts significantly as it cools, causing warping and bed adhesion failures.

Solutions:

  • Enclosure is highly recommended to maintain stable ambient temperature
  • Heated bed at 80-100°C
  • Specialized bed surfaces: Garolite (G10), PEI, or buildTak with glue stick
  • Large brims (15-25mm) for small contact area parts
  • Print slower first layers (15-20 mm/s)

⚠️ High Temperature Requirements

Problem: Nylon requires higher nozzle temperatures and may need printer upgrades.

Solutions:

  • All-metal hotend required for temperatures above 245°C
  • Capricorn or other high-temp PTFE tubes if below 245°C
  • Thermistor and heater cartridge rated for 300°C+
  • Hardened steel nozzle for carbon/glass fiber composites

⚠️ Essential Pre-requisites: You cannot successfully print nylon without: 1) A filament dryer, 2) An all-metal hotend, 3) A heated bed, and 4) Dry storage solutions. Attempting otherwise will waste material and time.

Optimal Printing Settings

Temperature Profile

Nozzle: 250-265°C (Pure Nylon)
Nozzle: 260-280°C (Carbon Fiber)
Bed: 80-100°C
Enclosure: 40-50°C ambient
No cooling fan or minimal (10-20%)

Print Speed

Default: 40-60 mm/s
First Layer: 15-25 mm/s
Outer Walls: 30-40 mm/s
Infill: 50-70 mm/s
Slower is generally better for quality

Retraction

Distance: 3-5mm (Direct)
Speed: 30-40 mm/s
Nylon strings moderately - reduce stringing with temperature tuning
Enable coasting and wiping

Bed Surface & Adhesion

Best: Garolite (G10)
Good: PEI with glue stick
Alternative: BuildTak with glue stick
Never: Bare glass

Pro Tip: Print a temperature tower for every new nylon spool, even from the same brand. Optimal temperature can vary by 10-15°C between different batches and colors.

Strengths of Nylon

  • Exceptional Strength & Durability: Highest impact resistance of common filaments, excellent fatigue resistance.
  • Outstanding Layer Adhesion: Layers fuse almost completely, creating nearly isotropic parts.
  • Excellent Chemical Resistance: Resistant to oils, fuels, solvents, and many chemicals.
  • Natural Flexibility: Can bend significantly without breaking - won't shatter like PLA.
  • Self-Lubricating: Low coefficient of friction, ideal for gears, bearings, and moving parts.
  • Good Heat Resistance: Higher than PETG, suitable for many functional applications.
  • Abrasion Resistant: Wears well in high-friction applications.
  • Can Be Dyed: Absorbs fabric dyes for custom colors post-printing.

Limitations & Considerations

  • Extreme Moisture Sensitivity: Requires continuous drying and dry storage - not a "print and forget" material.
  • Difficult to Print: Demands proper equipment and careful setup.
  • Warping Challenges: Large flat parts require expert techniques.
  • Higher Cost: Typically 2-3x more expensive than PLA.
  • Poor UV Resistance: Degrades in sunlight unless specially formulated.
  • Stringing: Prone to stringing, requiring careful retraction tuning.
  • Not Food Safe: Even food-grade nylon isn't recommended for prolonged food contact.
  • Odor During Printing: Some formulations have a distinct smell.

Ideal Applications

Nylon is the material of choice when performance matters more than ease of printing.

Functional Gears & Bearings

Low-friction, durable moving parts that outperform other plastics.

High-Stress Brackets & Mounts

Parts that undergo vibration, impact, or constant stress.

Drone & RC Car Parts

Lightweight, strong components that can survive crashes.

Wear-Resistant Components

Bushings, guides, and parts subject to friction and wear.

Tool Handles & Grips

Comfortable, durable grips that absorb vibration.

Automotive & Machinery Parts

Components exposed to oils, fuels, or mechanical stress.

Post-Processing & Finishing

Machining & Threading

Nylon machines beautifully - can be drilled, tapped, milled, and turned with standard metalworking tools.

Sanding & Polishing

Sands well with progressive grits. Can be polished to a high gloss with proper technique.

Dyeing

Unique to nylon - can be dyed with fabric dyes (Rit, iDye Poly) for permanent, vibrant colors throughout the part.

Annealing

Can be annealed in mineral oil or sand at 150-160°C to increase crystallinity, improving strength and heat resistance.

Dyeing Tip: For best results, dye nylon parts in near-boiling water with synthetic fabric dye for 10-30 minutes. The dye penetrates completely, coloring the entire part, not just the surface.
